# Assumptions and Approximations

## 1. Purpose

This document identifies assumptions and approximations that affect the interpretation of the TF results for CabotageLens. The model compares road-only transport with multimodal road-cabotage-road transport in Brazil, but it should not be presented as a perfect measurement system. The defensible academic claim is narrower: the assumptions are explicit, traceable where possible, and candidates for validation, sensitivity analysis, or limitation wording.

This inventory is based on the current repository documentation and implementation context, especially `docs/tf_document_structure.md`, `docs/tf_system_boundary.md`, `docs/tf_data_reliability_inventory.md`, `docs/methodology_audit.md`, `docs/mrv_container_efficiency.md`, `docs/hoteling_model.md`, `docs/port_ops_model.md`, `docs/costa_allocation_validation.md`, and `docs/tf.tex`. Where a specific runtime detail or parameter source is not fully traceable from the available documents, it is marked as uncertain or methodology debt rather than treated as established fact.

## 3. High-impact assumptions

### Maritime distance approximation

Sea distance is central because maritime fuel in transport-work mode is proportional to cargo mass, nautical distance, and fuel intensity. Distances from observed/corridor metadata are more defensible than simple geometric fallbacks, but unobserved pairs still need explicit confidence labeling. The thesis should distinguish matrix/observed distances from fallback haversine-style approximations.

### Vessel fuel intensity proxy

The strongest maritime pathway uses ANTAQ-observed cabotage legs enriched with EU MRV fuel-per-transport-work indicators. This is methodologically stronger than a generic class-average factor, but it is still a proxy: MRV is annual and external to the Brazilian route, and only part of the observed Brazilian cabotage fleet has matched usable MRV data. The thesis should report match rates, sample sizes, and whether each result used observed route intensity or fallback class intensity.

### Truck fuel consumption

The road model is deterministic and auditable, but it is not telematics. Because road emissions and costs scale directly with route distance and km/L, truck fuel assumptions can materially change modal savings. The TF should treat truck km/L as a sensitivity parameter rather than a single unquestioned truth.

### Empty backhaul

The active evaluator uses loaded road legs without empty backhaul. This is a clean shipment-level boundary, but it should not be interpreted as full fleet-cycle fuel use. If thesis conclusions discuss system-wide logistics emissions, empty repositioning must be included through sensitivity analysis or clearly excluded.

### Fuel prices

Fuel prices can dominate simplified cost outputs. Diesel and bunker prices are time-dependent, geography-dependent, and contract-dependent. Cost conclusions should be framed as energy-cost comparisons unless non-fuel logistics costs are added.

### Emission factors

The current model is primarily tank-to-wheel. That is acceptable for operational comparison, but not for claims about full lifecycle climate impact. Any discussion of alternative fuels, biodiesel, HVO, LNG, electricity, or shore power should avoid well-to-wake claims unless upstream factors are added.

### Cost boundary

Cabotage competitiveness is not determined by fuel cost alone. Port tariffs, inventory cost, time, service frequency, reliability, tolls, labor, and commercial freight rates can reverse or weaken a purely energy-cost conclusion. The thesis should explicitly call the current cost output a simplified energy/operations boundary.

### Hoteling

Hoteling can be important, but it is also a double-counting risk when the maritime input is an aggregate transport-work intensity. The TF should document the conditional logic: separate hoteling is appropriate for class/fallback decomposition, but not automatically additive when the selected maritime intensity already represents observed operational fuel.

## 4. Methodology debts

- Verify the exact provenance and calibration of every maritime distance in `data/sea_matrix.json`, including which pairs are observed, matrix-entered, corridor-derived, or fallback-generated.
- Produce a small validation sample comparing API road distances with known carrier, Google Maps, DNIT, or benchmark workbook road distances for thesis OD pairs.
- Define a thesis-grade port selection rule beyond nearest-port geometry, or clearly label nearest-port selection as a heuristic.
- Build sensitivity cases for truck km/L, payload/load factor, and empty backhaul share.
- Build sensitivity cases for VLSFO/bunker price and diesel price, with the price date/source shown in result tables.
- Verify and cite the emission factors used for diesel and VLSFO, including whether they are CO2-only or CO2e and whether they are tank-to-wheel only.
- Decide how to present port cargo-handling emissions: included with placeholders, excluded from primary results, or used only as a sensitivity/decomposition item.
- Confirm when hoteling is included versus excluded, and ensure thesis tables expose the exclusion reason to avoid double counting confusion.
- Document data coverage for each maritime result: route match rate, matched voyages, matched IMOs, segment count, and whether route/corridor/class fallback was used.
- Create a reproducibility manifest for final thesis runs: code version, input parameters, data artifact versions, route cache status, provider profile, and output timestamp.
- Clarify whether cost results are energy-cost-only, energy plus port operations, or a broader logistics-cost proxy before using economic language in conclusions.
- Validate the default tonnes-per-TEU assumption against the benchmark cargo and any thesis case-study commodity assumptions.

## 5. Recommended thesis wording

The following wording can be adapted for the methodology or limitations chapter.

> The model is designed as a reproducible comparative estimator rather than a direct measurement of each shipment. Its outputs depend on explicit assumptions regarding routing, port selection, fuel consumption, fuel prices, emission factors, and the boundary of included logistics costs.

> Road distances are obtained from routing-provider geometry and cache records, not from observed truck GPS trajectories. Therefore, the road component should be interpreted as a standardized route estimate under a defined provider profile, not as a reconstruction of individual carrier behavior.

> For the maritime leg, the preferred formulation uses route-level fuel intensity derived from observed ANTAQ cabotage movements matched to EU MRV fuel-per-transport-work indicators. Where this observed route intensity is unavailable, the model relies on class-level or fallback approximations, which are reported as lower-confidence estimates.

> The environmental boundary is predominantly tank-to-wheel. The results therefore represent direct operational combustion emissions and should not be interpreted as a complete well-to-wake or lifecycle assessment.

> Cost outputs are limited to fuel and simplified operational components represented in the model. They should not be read as full freight rates or total logistics costs unless tolls, port tariffs, inventory cost, service frequency, labor, insurance, and commercial freight terms are added explicitly.

> The nearest-port rule is a geometric heuristic used to generate deterministic multimodal alternatives. In practice, port choice also depends on service availability, frequency, contractual arrangements, terminal cost, equipment availability, transit time, and reliability. This limitation is especially relevant when interpreting corridors where the nearest port is not necessarily the commercially feasible port.

> Assumptions with potentially high influence on conclusions, such as maritime distance, vessel fuel intensity, truck fuel consumption, empty backhaul, fuel prices, emission factors, cost boundary, and hoteling treatment, should be tested through sensitivity analysis before final numerical conclusions are frozen.
